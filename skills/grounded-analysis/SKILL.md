# Grounded Analysis

Attribution: created by Vitali Gusatinsky / DesignAnswers.

Most bad analysis isn't low-effort — it's *confident and ungrounded*. An agent (or a person) generates an
insight-shaped claim — a contrarian reframe, a "you led with the flashy option," a tidy ranking — and then
back-fills the fact that makes it work, instead of checking the source first. **The narrative recruits the
"fact."** The result sounds sharp and is wrong, and one wrong critique poisons trust in all the analysis
around it.

This skill exists to keep analysis sharp *and* honest. It does not make analysis timid — contrarian and
critical reads are the point. It makes them earn their place: a critique ships with the line that proves
it, or it ships labeled as a hypothesis, or it doesn't ship.

> **Worked failure (anonymized).** Asked to analyze a mentoring session, an agent wrote: "you led with the
> flashy use case because it demos best." The transcript showed the opposite — the *mentor* proposed the
> practical option and the *participant* redirected to the other one because she'd already started it. The
> disconfirming line was one read away. The agent had the data; it just never checked the claim, because
> the consultant-y "you led with the flashy one" shape *felt* like insight. That is the failure this skill
> prevents.

## Interaction model — adaptive

- **Clear, low-stakes ask → one-shot.** Analyze immediately. State the assumptions you're making out loud
  ("assuming you mean the session from the 23rd, and that 'it worked' means adoption, not just task
  completion"), and let the person correct.
- **High-stakes or ambiguous → frame first.** Pause for 2–3 scoping questions before analyzing when it's a
  performance review, a consequential decision, an unfamiliar subject, or the source/audience is unclear.
  The three questions worth asking: *what decision does this feed · what sources exist · who is it for.*
- Default to one-shot for speed; escalate to frame-first only when getting it wrong is expensive.

## The method (every mode)

### 1. Frame
Name the question, the decision it feeds, and what a useful answer would actually *change*. Identify the
source material. If you're inferring the question rather than being told it, say so.

### 2. Ground — pull the source before interpreting
Do not analyze from memory or impression. Go to the actual material first: the transcript, the document,
the notes, the data, the prior record.
- Read the **full** source, not a skim.
- For any transcript or multi-party record, **trace attribution at every decision point** — who proposed,
  who chose, who pushed back, who conceded. Do **not** default to the stereotype ("the facilitator drives
  the agenda," "the senior person decided"). This is precisely where ungrounded critiques are born.
- Extract the verbatim evidence (quotes, lines, figures) that any later claim will rest on.

### 3. Observe → evaluate
Build the **factual spine first** — what is verifiably present, with citations. *Then* layer judgment on
top. Tag every claim by confidence:
- **[confirmed]** — backed by a quote, a line, a record. Cite it inline.
- **[inferred]** — a reasonable read from the evidence, not stated outright. Marked as such.
- **[speculative]** — a hypothesis worth testing. Marked, never dressed up as fact.

A critique stated as fact must be **[confirmed]**-tier, with the evidence shown.

### 4. Adversarial gate (load-bearing — do not skip)
Before delivering, run every sharp, critical, or contrarian claim through these checks:
- [ ] **What would make this false?** Look for the disconfirmer *in the source*. The falsifier is usually
      one search away.
- [ ] **Did I assume attribution?** Re-verify who actually said, decided, or chose the thing.
- [ ] **Did a satisfying narrative recruit a fact?** If the take arrived first and the evidence second,
      treat it as suspect until proven.
- [ ] **Is any evaluation ungrounded?** Every "this was weak / strong / wrong" needs its cited fact.
- [ ] **Frequency ≠ importance.** Weigh each signal by how much it matters to the decision-maker's actual
      goals, not by how often it came up.

Anything that doesn't survive is cut or downgraded to **[speculative]**. Keep the sharpness — ground it.
Don't retreat into hedged mush; a grounded critique is the product, an ungrounded one is the failure.

### 5. Deliver
- Lead with the single most load-bearing read, grounded.
- Keep confidence visible — use the tags where it matters, not on every sentence.
- Critiques stay sharp **and** cited.
- Close with **"so what"** (the decision or next lever) and **"what would change my mind"** (the
  disconfirming evidence you'd look for, or the thing you couldn't verify).

## Domain adapters (auto-detect the mode)

- **Meeting / mentoring session.** Ground in the transcript and any prior records. Read: who drove the
  agenda, what was taught or proposed vs. what actually *landed*, the other party's level and posture, and
  the single most useful next lever. Attribution discipline matters most here.
- **Ideation.** Steelman the idea first, then run a premortem. Separate what's genuinely new from what's
  already known. Name the cheapest test that would validate or kill it.
- **Planning.** Check feasibility against reality — dependencies, what it needs, what's gated or blocked.
  Compare scope against the stated goal and flag creep. Name what could go wrong and its earliest signal.
- **Performance** (a person's, a team's, or your own). Judge against an explicit baseline, not vibes.
  **Trajectory over snapshot** — one good or bad instance is not the trend; resist recency bias. This mode
  is high-stakes, so frame-first by default and keep it private unless told otherwise.
- **Decision / strategy.** Lay out the real options, the trade-off actually being made, the second-order
  consequences, reversibility, and opportunity cost. Challenge the premise before scoring the options.

## Output handling

- **About a client, project, person, or shared engagement →** route the finished analysis into the
  relevant system of record (project tracker, CRM, notes, knowledge base) so it's not lost. Cross-link the
  source it was built from.
- **Self / private / exploratory →** keep it in the conversation; persist nothing unless asked.
- **Never auto-send or auto-file anything outward** (issue trackers, email, client-facing messages).
  Draft it; let the human confirm before it goes anywhere.

## Anti-patterns (do not do)

- **Don't state an insight before grounding it.** If the take arrived before the evidence, it's suspect
  until checked.
- **Don't assume who drove a decision in a multi-party record.** Trace the turns.
- **Don't present a quote and an assumption at the same confidence.** Tag them.
- **Don't dramatize a risk the person is already managing.** Check whether they flagged or mitigated it
  themselves before warning them about it.
- **Don't go sycophantic to play it safe.** Contrarian and critical takes are wanted — grounded ones.
- **Don't rank by frequency.** How often something recurred is not how much it matters.
- **Don't analyze from memory.** Pull the source first.
